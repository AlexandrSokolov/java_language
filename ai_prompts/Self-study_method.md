# The Method: Learning by Interrogating Your Own Understanding

*A self-study system for learning something deeply and keeping it.*

---

## The core idea

You maintain your own set of question/answer cards on whatever you are learning. You write the
**question** yourself, from your own understanding. Later you try to answer it from memory, then
reveal the stored answer and check yourself.

The twist that makes it work: **you author the questions, never outsource them.** A question someone
else wrote (or a tool generated) is one you *recognize*. A question you wrote from your own grasp of
the topic is one you must genuinely *reconstruct*. Recognition is not knowledge. Reconstruction is.

## Why it works (the motivation)

Three forces are doing the work, and most study methods use at most one of them:

1. **Active recall.** Pulling an answer out of your head — not re-reading it — is what actually
   strengthens memory. Reading feels productive and mostly isn't. Retrieving is uncomfortable and
   is where the learning happens.

2. **Forgetting as an instrument, not an enemy.** You will forget things. Most methods fight that.
   This one *uses* it: when you fail to recall a card, that failure is a signal pointing exactly at
   what you no longer understand. The system is *anti-fragile to forgetting* — forgetting tells you
   where to look, instead of quietly eroding you.

3. **Metacognition — interrogating your own understanding.** The real habit underneath the cards is
   one question asked constantly: *"Do I actually know this, or do I just recognize it?"* That
   reflex is the durable skill. The cards are only its vehicle.

## The one rule that took the longest to learn

**One question, one axis.** A question that secretly asks about three things at once tests none of
them well — and worse, when you can't answer it cleanly you can't tell *why*. Was it a real gap, or
just that the question's shape was ambiguous (how many points? which angle? what was being asked)?

So a failed recall is treated as a **possible bug in the question, not only a gap in knowledge.**
If a card is hard to answer cleanly, the first suspicion is: *is this one question, or several fused
into one?* If several — split it. One card per dimension, each with an answer whose shape follows
from that single dimension.

This inversion — suspect the question first — is the heart of the method. It keeps the signal clean:
a failure now genuinely means you didn't know the thing.

## How to write good cards (in short)

- **Single axis.** If a concept has several dimensions, that is several cards, not one card with a
  multi-part answer.
- **No hints in the question.** The question names the *problem or context*, never the answer's key
  words. A question that telegraphs its answer trains recognition, which is worthless here.
- **Scaffold, don't list.** Where you must remember several things, organize them so they
  *reconstruct* — a causal chain ("each step forces the next"), a derivation from one root idea, or
  a single labeled spectrum — rather than a flat list of unrelated items to memorize.
- **Link cards together.** Let a high-level "what are the main concerns of X?" card route into the
  detailed ones. The map should be walkable.
- **Two kinds of card are both fine, but keep them distinct:** open *"what should you consider…"*
  questions that probe your mental model, and closed *"how do you achieve X?"* questions with one
  right answer. Don't blur them.

## How it compares to other methods

- **vs. re-reading / highlighting.** Those feel productive but mostly build familiarity, not
  recall. This method forces retrieval, which is what actually sticks.
- **vs. ordinary flashcards / Anki.** Same spaced-recall engine, but most flashcard users either
  download decks or generate them. Here, *writing the question is half the learning* — it forces you
  to decide what the real question even is. The deck is a by-product of thinking, not a thing you
  consume.
- **vs. note-taking / second-brain systems.** Notes are for *finding* things later. This is for
  *holding* them in your head. Different goal: a note you can look up is not knowledge you possess.
- **vs. passive courses / videos.** They transmit; they don't verify. This method's whole point is
  verification — it tells you, ruthlessly and specifically, what you don't yet know.

## For teaching children (a note to my future self)

The full card system fits once they have a body of knowledge worth maintaining — roughly the teen
years. But the deeper thing starts much earlier and is the real inheritance:

> Teach them to ask, of anything they think they've learned:
> **"Could I explain this to someone who doesn't know it?"**

That is the same muscle, sized down — interrogating your own understanding instead of trusting the
warm feeling of having seen something before. The flashcards are just where that habit grows up.
